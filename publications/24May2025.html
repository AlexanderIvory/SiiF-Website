Enterprise Value vs. Equity Value: Interview Guide

May 24, 2025

"Why do we look at both Enterprise Value and Equity Value?" This question frequently appears in finance interviews to test your understanding of fundamental valuation concepts. This guide provides a concise answer and essential insights to help you demonstrate your knowledge.

Interview-Ready Sample Answer

"Enterprise Value represents the value of the company that is attributable to all investors; Equity Value only represents the portion available to shareholders (equity investors). You look at both because Equity Value is the number the public-at-large sees, while Enterprise Value represents the company's true total value. Enterprise Value is crucial for comparing companies with different capital structures and is used with metrics like EBITDA that benefit all capital providers. Meanwhile, Equity Value is important for understanding shareholder returns and is used with metrics like net income that flow to equity holders."

Breakdown of Concepts

Enterprise Value (EV)

The total value of a company attributable to all capital providers (debt holders, preferred shareholders, equity holders).

Formula: $\text{EV} = \text{Equity Value} + \text{Debt} + \text{Preferred Stock} + \text{Noncontrolling Interest} - \text{Cash}$

Used with: EBITDA, EBIT, Revenue, NOPAT (metrics that benefit all capital providers)

Equity Value

The value of a company attributable to common shareholders.

Formula: $\text{Equity Value} = \text{Share Price} \times \text{Shares Outstanding}$

Also known as Market Capitalization (for public companies).

Used with: Net Income, EPS (metrics that benefit equity holders)

Key Components of Enterprise Value

Debt:

All interest-bearing liabilities (short and long-term debt).

Cash:

Subtracted because it reduces the effective cost of acquiring the company.

Cash is a non-operating asset that could be used to pay down debt.

Preferred Stock:

Has priority over common stock but not debt; included as it's a claim on the business.

Noncontrolling Interest:

Value of subsidiaries not wholly owned by the company.

Valuation Multiple Applications

Enterprise Value Multiples:

  • EV/EBITDA
  • EV/Revenue
  • EV/EBIT

Equity Value Multiples:

  • P/E (Price-to-Earnings)
  • P/B (Price-to-Book)

Why It's Asked

This question tests your grasp of valuation fundamentals and your ability to distinguish between different investor claims. It also reveals whether you understand which value is appropriate for different use cases (e.g., comparable company analysis vs. shareholder returns). Understanding these concepts is essential for accurate financial analysis and valuation.

Bonus Tip

Insight: Enterprise and Equity Value are two sides of the same coin. Interviewers want to test whether you understand the logic of valuation inputs and can explain when each is appropriate.

When transitioning from Enterprise Value to Equity Value: EV – Debt – Preferred Stock – Noncontrolling Interest + Cash = Equity Value

"Common Mistake: Mixing up EV and Equity Value when applying valuation multiples — e.g., using Equity Value with EBITDA is incorrect because EBITDA benefits all capital providers, not just equity holders."

Follow-Up Questions to Prepare For

  • What's the formula for Enterprise Value?
  • Why do we subtract cash when calculating EV?
  • Why do we add Preferred Stock or Noncontrolling Interest to EV?
  • How do you calculate fully diluted shares?
  • Could a company have negative Enterprise or Equity Value?
  • What's the difference between Equity Value and Shareholders' Equity?

Preparing for Your Interview

Understanding the relationship between Enterprise Value and Equity Value is fundamental to finance interviews. Be prepared to explain not just the formulas but also the rationale behind using each metric in different contexts. Remember that interviewers are testing your conceptual understanding and ability to apply these concepts in practical valuation scenarios.
